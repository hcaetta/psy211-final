Biological Factors

Biological factors are internal influences on an individual's health, behavior, and development. Biological factors include genetics, brain chemistry, and hormones. These factors help shape behavior, mental health, and physical conditions. In regards to criminal behavior, biological factors are most prominently related to antisocial behaviors, mental health conditions, and genetic dispositions.

Fear Conditioning

Fear conditioning is how the brain connects actions with negative consequences. For those with poorly developed fear conditioning, it may make it harder to feel guilt, shame, or anxiety after criminal behavior. An individual with poorly developed fear conditioning will not always commit crime, but it may impact how they respond to punishment. This can help explain why some people continue impulsive or antisocial behavior even when consequences are apparent.

Brain Function

Brain function can influence impulse control, decision-making, and emotional regulation. Differences in brain areas related to judgment and emotions can make it harder to stop and think before acting. When the brain struggles to regulate emotions, poor decisions may be made resulting in aggressive or impulsive behavior. Brain function is only one part of behavior, and it is shaped by environment, experiences. and development.

Genetic Influences

Genetic influences are related to hereditary and genes; genetics may influence traits like impulsivity, agression, or sensitivity to stress. A person's genes do not directly cause criminal behavior. Genetic susceptibility is increased when combined with difficult environments, trauma, or lack of community. Biology and environment work together and not separately.

Myth vs. Fact

Myth: There is a "warrior gene" that makes people violet or criminal.

Fact: The MAOA gene may be linked to agression or criminal behavior, but it does not determine if someone will commit crime or not. Research suggests that genetics only become significant when combined with environmental factors, such as childhood trauma or lack of support.

Takeaway

Biological factors can help explain why some people may be more vulnerable to impilsive or antisocial behavior, but they do not determine a person's choices. Nothing is set in stone; criminal behavior is also influenced by social and psychological factors.

Psychological Factors

Psychological factors focus on how thoughts, emotions, and behavior patterns can influence an individuals actions. They can affect how a person handles stress, anger, fear, and frustration. They can also include things such as self-control, impulse control, agression, and mental-health-related conditions. These factors may increase risk, but do not fully explain criminal behavior by itself.

Emotional Regulation

Emotional dysregulation refers to the inability to control and manage intnse negative emotions. This factor is a key psychological driver of crime, contributing to agression, violent acts, and criminal behavior. Problems with emotional functioning are typically associated with later criminal behavior; specifically, family psychsocial prblems and individual mental health problems were linked with criminality for both females and males.

Impulse Control

Poor impulse control is characterized by the inability to resist harmful urges, often leading to criminal behavior. It involves failure to resist an impulse to perform an act that is dangerous to oneself or others. Weak response to consequences can reduce the "pause and think" process behind self-control. Drugs and alcohol, which inhibit impulse control, are also known to increase the likelihood to engage in criminal behavior.

Agression and Antisocial Traits

Agression and antisocial traits are related to Antisocial Personality Disorder (ASPD), characterized by a disgard for others, norms, and safety. Individuals showing these traits often have behaviors of violence, impulsivity, cruetly, and lack of remorse. Amygdala dysfunction is linked to antisocial behavior, expressing poor fear condiyioning, reduced responses to emotional stimuli, and problems recognizing fearful facial expressions. Some studies have found increased amygda;a responsiveness in antisocial individuals and in people with a genetic predisposition to agressive behavior, suggesting that aggression can emerge through both psychological and developmental routes.

Myth vs. Fact

Myth: People commit crimes because of one psychological cause.

Fact: Criminal behavior develops through multiple interacting influences, including mental health conditions, substance use, and other family psychosocial risks.

Takeaway

Understanding psychological factors can help identify risk factors earlier on and support prevention of criminal behavior. The findings point to the importance of addressing mental health conditions, substance use, and other risk pathaways when trying to reduce criminal behavior.
